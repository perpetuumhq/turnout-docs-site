1. Product Doctrine

What Turnout is, what it is not, and the conviction thesis that drives every decision.

Strategic Reframe: Venue Demand Operating System

Turnout should not be built as an AI ad generator. It should be built as an AI venue demand operating system, where ad generation is the sharpest weapon in the stack.

That distinction matters because an ad generator is easy to compare. A venue demand OS is hard to replace.

What Turnout Is

Turnout is the system that helps a venue answer one brutal question every week:

"What should we say, to whom, in which format, for which daypart, with which offer, on which channel, to fill this space profitably?"

The product is not one tool. It is six linked capabilities:

  • Venue understanding — who you are, what you sell, who comes, what times matter
  • Campaign planning — what to run this week, why, and for whom
  • Creative generation — truthful, format-native ads from real venue assets
  • WhatsApp conversion — inquiry to reservation in a single stateful thread
  • Attribution and learning — which ad caused which walk-in, and what to do next
  • Commercial primitives — proper booking, deposit, pack, and coordination infrastructure for each category

What Turnout Is Not

  • A Canva clone for venue owners
  • A generic creative studio
  • A listing card with AI frosting
  • A horizontal discovery layer without commercial conviction
  • A dashboard-heavy SaaS that asks operators to learn marketing

Product Definition (One Line)

Existing venue photos become Instagram ads, leads go to WhatsApp, every walk-in is attributed back to the ad, and closure happens through properly designed commercial primitives suited to the category.

The Conviction Thesis

India's go-out economy is shifting from posting-led behavior to presence-led behavior. People increasingly want experiences that feel real, social, and worth the effort.

Turnout's job is not to show every option; it is to manufacture conviction to leave the couch and show up.

This is not a listing product. This is a conviction product.

2. Moat Thesis: The 5-Layer Closed-Loop Venue Demand Graph

The competitive advantage: a venue knowledge system that gets stronger with every outcome.

Layer 1: Venue Knowledge Graph

Ingest venue reality across 8 dimensions:

  • Visual DNA (photos, color, spatial layout, visual motifs)
  • Category signals (primary, secondary, implicit themes)
  • Audience hypotheses (who actually comes, peak times, segment patterns)
  • Vibe tags (social, intense, casual, premium, etc.)
  • Operating schedule and daypart structure
  • Pricing and commercial structure
  • Social signals (IG engagement, review sentiment, follower quality)
  • Competitive context (nearby venues, benchmarks, market saturation)

Layer 2: Creative Genome

Every piece of content generated is tagged with:

  • Format DNA (static/carousel/reel/story/collection)
  • Visual motif (which venue assets, which color palette, which props)
  • Narrative hook (what story it tells: social proof, FOMO, community, skill, discovery)
  • Audience target (who this is for, based on visitor persona)
  • Truthfulness tier (T1 real, T2 real+assisted, T3 conceptual)
  • Engagement signal (CTR, completion rate, share rate)

Layer 3: Conversion Graph

Track every inquiry → conversation → booking → show-up → repeat:

  • Which ad drove the inquiry
  • What friction was detected in the landing page
  • Which concierge persona handled it
  • What offer closed it
  • Whether the user showed up
  • Whether they came back

Layer 4: Benchmark Graph

Performance comparison across:

  • Same venue, last week (trend)
  • Peer venues in same city (relative performance)
  • Theme-wide benchmarks (what good looks like for sports-social)
  • Time-of-week patterns (should this daypart get more spend?)
  • Price elasticity signals (can we charge more?)

Layer 5: Operator Memory

Long-term learning about what works for this specific venue:

  • Which audiences convert best (and why)
  • Which offers close highest (and at what elasticity)
  • Which narratives resonate (based on creative performance)
  • Which channels work (Instagram vs. referral vs. organic)
  • Which times are weak vs. strong (and seasonal patterns)

3. Truthful Creative Principle: 3 Tiers

What we create, and the permission structure for different truthfulness levels.

Tier 1: Real (T1)

Definition: Unmodified photos and videos of the venue, products, people, and experiences.

What's allowed:

  • Existing venue photos (cropped, color-corrected, but not AI-altered)
  • Real video of real events/customers/staff
  • Honest captions about real offerings
  • Real testimonials from real customers

What's forbidden:

  • AI-generated faces or bodies
  • Photoshopped people or objects
  • False claims about availability, pricing, or service
  • AI-upsampled images presented as photography

Badge: Verified checkmark. High trust, slower to produce.

Tier 2: Real + Assisted (T2)

Definition: Real venue assets enhanced with AI assistance (upsampling, composition, style transfer), clearly marked as AI-assisted.

What's allowed:

  • Real photos AI-upsampled or color-enhanced
  • Real objects composited into realistic scenes (e.g., real dish on a styled table)
  • Real venue sections recombined with AI for variety
  • Style transfer on real photos (e.g., warm filter applied consistently)
  • Missing elements added with AI (e.g., fill a dark corner with light realism)

What's forbidden:

  • AI-generated people, crowds, or faces
  • Realistic objects that don't exist in the venue
  • False claims about what's available or real
  • Hiding the AI-assisted tag

Badge: "AI-Assisted by Turnout" watermark. Faster, good trust.

Tier 3: Conceptual (T3)

Definition: Fully AI-generated creative, based on venue knowledge and brief, for ideation only.

When used:

  • Exploring creative directions before shooting
  • Visualizing event concepts for approval
  • Filling content gaps during cold start
  • Never as primary customer-facing asset without venue approval

Badge: "Generated by AI" watermark. Lower trust, but speeds experimentation.

4. Trust × Taste Graph: Venue-Level Scoring & Template Recommendation

The core algorithm that drives template recommendation. Scores every venue on trust (data quality) and taste (audience fit), then recommends the optimal page template.

Axis 1: Trust Score (T1-T6)

How much real venue data do we have? 6 components:

Component Signal Scoring Weight
T1: Content Readiness IG photo count + quality + Google gallery + menu availability 0-25 (threshold: 12+ for launch) 25%
T2: Commercial Clarity Pricing on website + booking system + Zomato data + menu structure 0-25 (threshold: 15+ for launch) 25%
T3: Category Confidence IG captions + reviews + owner answers + category clarity 0-15 (is this sports-social or dining or wellness?) 15%

Template Recommendation Algorithm

Pseudo-code for recommend_template(venue_profile):


function recommend_template(venue_data):
    trust_score = calculate_trust(venue_data)
    taste_vector = classify_themes(venue_data)

    primary_theme = taste_vector.argmax()
    theme_confidence = taste_vector[primary_theme] / 100

    secondary_themes = taste_vector.top_k(2)[1:]  # themes 2-3

    IF trust_score >= 70:
        confidence = "HIGH"
        recommendation_basis = "Rich data"
    ELSE IF trust_score >= 40:
        confidence = "MEDIUM"
        recommendation_basis = "Partial data, conservative choice"
    ELSE:
        confidence = "LOW"
        recommendation_basis = "Content-driven only"

    template = TEMPLATE_MAP[primary_theme]
    overrides = []

    // Theme blending: if confidence in primary < 70% and secondary exists
    IF theme_confidence < 0.70 AND secondary_themes[0] > 50:
        overrides.append("blend_secondary_hero")
        overrides.append("dual_CTA_zones")

    // Content adaptation: if trust is LOW, request more assets
    IF trust_score < 40:
        overrides.append("request_content_first")
        overrides.append("use_default_hero_placeholder")

    // City overlay always applied
    city_overlay = CITY_OVERLAYS[venue_data.city]

    return {
        template: template,
        confidence: confidence,
        basis: recommendation_basis,
        overrides: overrides,
        city_overlay: city_overlay,
        theme_blend: secondary_themes,
        trust_score: trust_score,
        taste_vector: taste_vector
    }

5. Product Architecture: 8 Layers + LaunchPad Runtime

The full system architecture, from venue ingestion through owner operations and consumer outcome.

LaunchPad Runtime Loop (5 Steps)

Every page generation and concierge interaction follows this cycle:

  1. Consented Input: Owner provides Instagram handle, website, Zomato/District access (pre-launch)
  2. AI Page Composition: System generates landing page from venue profile + campaign brief
  3. Friction Detection: Page is instrumented; user behavior is scanned for decision friction
  4. WA Concierge Handoff: If friction detected, WhatsApp persona-aware concierge is triggered
  5. Closure & Proof: Booking made, reservation tracked, walk-in attributed, learning captured

8-Layer Architecture

Layer Responsibility Input Output
Layer 0: Venue Ingestion Ingest venue reality (photos, reviews, pricing, schedule, IG, website, Zomato) IG handle, website URL, Zomato link, owner answers Venue knowledge graph (70 signals)

Layer 2: Creative Generation Generate format-native ads (static, carousel, reel, story, collection) at T1/T2/T3 Venue assets, campaign brief, audience target, format 5 ad formats, all tiers, all personas

6. Page Composition Engine: Algorithm & Rules

How the system generates a landing page from template, campaign, creative, and city context. This is where the system becomes a product.

Content Slot Architecture

Every page is composed of named slots:

  • hero_image — Primary hero (1200×600px, optimized for theme + audience)
  • hero_headline — Primary message (theme-native copy)
  • hero_subheadline — Secondary message (urgency or benefit)
  • trust_block — Social proof + verification (reviews, attendance, credentials)
  • category_section — What this venue is (with adaptive badges: Sports-Social, Beginner-Friendly, etc.)
  • primary_offer — Lead offer (price, package, incentive)
  • secondary_offers — Alternative offers (bundles, packs, discounts)
  • pricing_section — Full pricing transparency (base, off-peak, packages)
  • adaptive_content_zones — Persona-specific content (auto-hidden/shown based on page detection)
  • media_gallery — Real venue photos (T1/T2 tagged)
  • faq_zone — Friction-specific FAQs (auto-populated based on venue theme + owner answers)
  • cta_button — "Message on WhatsApp" with WA pre-fill
  • trust_badge — Truthful Creative tier indicator (T1/T2/T3)
  • creator_attribution — Photo credits, creator links
  • share_card — Open Graph meta (for IG, WA sharing)

Composition Algorithm: compose_page()

Pseudo-code:


function compose_page(venue_id, campaign_brief, template_id, city):
    // Step 1: Load base template
    template = TEMPLATES[template_id]  // e.g., "sports-social"

    // Step 2: Load venue profile & campaign context
    venue = VENUE_DB[venue_id]
    campaign = campaign_brief
    city_overlay = CITY_OVERLAYS[city]

    // Step 3: Inject primitives (booking, pricing, offers)
    primitive_bundle = TEMPLATE_PRIMITIVE_BUNDLES[template_id]
    // Determines which primitives to use (slots, reservations, split-pay, etc.)

    // Step 4: Select creative assets
    hero_image = select_hero(venue.assets, campaign.audience, template)
    creative_variants = generate_or_fetch_ads(
        venue=venue,
        campaign=campaign,
        formats=["static"],  // for page hero
        tier=campaign.creative_tier  // T1/T2/T3
    )
    hero_headline = creative_variants[0].headline
    hero_subheadline = creative_variants[0].cta_copy

    // Step 5: Apply campaign overrides
    primary_offer = campaign.recommended_offer
    secondary_offers = campaign.alternative_offers
    pricing = apply_offer_to_pricing(venue.pricing, primary_offer)

    // Step 6: Apply city character overlay
    tone = city_overlay.tone  // e.g., "abundance" for Delhi
    layout_hints = city_overlay.layout_hints

    // Step 7: Build adaptive content zones
    adaptive_zones = {}
    FOR each persona in PERSONAS_FOR_TEMPLATE[template]:
        adaptive_zones[persona] = {
            headline_variant: variant_for_persona(persona, hero_headline),
            offer_variant: variant_for_persona(persona, primary_offer),
            faq_items: faq_for_persona(persona, venue),
            cta_text: cta_for_persona(persona, template)
        }

    // Step 8: Populate slots
    page.hero_image = hero_image
    page.hero_headline = hero_headline
    page.hero_subheadline = hero_subheadline
    page.trust_block = build_trust_block(venue, template)
    page.category_section = build_category_section(venue, template)
    page.primary_offer = primary_offer
    page.secondary_offers = secondary_offers
    page.pricing_section = pricing
    page.adaptive_content_zones = adaptive_zones
    page.media_gallery = venue.assets.photos[0:9]
    page.faq_zone = faq_for_template(template, venue)
    page.cta_button = build_wa_cta(venue)
    page.trust_badge = build_tier_badge(campaign.creative_tier)
    page.creator_attribution = venue.assets.credits
    page.share_card = build_og_meta(venue, template, hero_image)

    // Step 9: Add instrumentation for friction detection
    page = add_instrumentation(page, {
        track_scroll: true,
        track_clicks: true,
        track_form_abandon: true,
        friction_events: ["pricing_scroll", "offer_hesitation", "faq_view", "group_confusion"]
    })

    // Step 10: Add learning variant marker (for A/B testing)
    page.variant_id = generate_variant_id(venue_id, campaign.id, template, city)
    page.learning_signals = ["ctr", "page_time", "scroll_depth", "friction_trigger", "wa_conversion"]

    return page

function select_hero(assets, audience, template):
    // Rank assets by: relevance to audience + alignment with template + T1 quality
    candidates = assets.filter(a => a.tier == "T1" OR a.tier == "T2")
    ranked = candidates.sort_by([
        relevance_to_audience,
        template_affinity,
        aesthetic_quality,
        freshness
    ])
    return ranked[0]

function build_trust_block(venue, template):
    return {
        google_rating: venue.rating,
        review_count: venue.review_count,
        attendance_signal: venue.monthly_visitors,
        social_proof: venue.instagram_followers,
        verified_badge: venue.trust_score > 70,
        audience_testimonial: random_real_review(venue),
        recurring_customer_count: venue.repeat_rate_pct
    }

function build_category_section(venue, template):
    badges = []
    IF template == "sports-social":
        badges.append("Court Booking")
        badges.append("Equipment Available")
        IF venue.beginner_friendly: badges.append("Beginner-Friendly")
    ELSE IF template == "brunch-cafe":
        badges.append("Reservations")
        badges.append("Groups Welcome")
        badges.append("Open Weekends")

    return {
        primary_label: venue.primary_category,
        secondary_labels: venue.secondary_categories,
        badges: badges,
        description: venue.about_snippet
    }

Fallback Logic

  • If hero image is T-LOW quality → use placeholder with clear badge "This space is waiting for photos"
  • If pricing data is missing → show "Contact on WhatsApp for pricing"
  • If reviews are sparse → emphasize "New venue, verified booking"
  • If campaign brief conflicts with venue data → campaign brief wins (owner override)

7. Persona Multiplexing System: Two-Layer Detection

One landing page serves 4-7 distinct personas. The system detects which persona is visiting at two layers: on the page (before WA) and in the first WA message (to confirm/correct).

The Problem: Why 1:1 Template → Concierge Fails

A Sports-Social page doesn't just serve "Group Coordinators." It serves:

  • Beginners — need reassurance, gear info, low-commitment entry
  • Regulars — need fast rebooking, pack purchase, schedule visibility
  • Solo Players — need open-play discovery and social matching
  • Corporate Event Planners — need bulk booking, invoicing, team coordination
  • Parents with Kids — need age-appropriate sessions, safety info, family pricing
  • Price-Sensitive Students — need off-peak deals, discounts, trial offers

A one-size-fits-all concierge would mishandle 5 of these 6. Every template generates 4-7 distinct visitor personas. Shipping without detecting this is a one-size-fits-none experience.

Two-Layer Persona Detection Architecture


LAYER 1: PAGE-LEVEL DETECTION (before WA)
Signals: referrer, time-of-day, device, scroll behavior, section dwell,
         CTA interactions, booking flow choices, group-intent signals
↓
Output: persona_hypothesis {type, confidence, signals_used[]}
↓
Page adapts: Surfaces persona-relevant content (adaptive zones),
             adjusts CTA language, primes WA handoff context

LAYER 2: WA ENTRY CLASSIFICATION (first 1-2 messages)
Input: persona_hypothesis from page + user's first message
↓
Classifier: Pattern match on first message + page context
↓
Output: persona_confirmed {type, confidence, override_from_page?}
↓
Concierge branches: Selects persona-specific conversation tree,
                    tone, knowledge depth, closure strategy

LAYER 2.5: MID-CONVERSATION RE-CLASSIFICATION
Trigger: User reveals new intent mid-conversation
(e.g., starts as beginner question, pivots to group booking)
↓
Concierge re-classifies and pivots gracefully

Pseudo-code for classify_wa_persona(page_hypothesis, first_message):


function classify_wa_persona(page_hypothesis, user_first_message):
    // Input: persona_hypothesis from page + first WA message from user
    
    persona_prior = page_hypothesis.type
    prior_confidence = page_hypothesis.confidence
    
    // Parse first message
    message_patterns = {
        "group": ["me and my friends", "how many", "can we get", "organize", "group"],
        "beginner": ["first time", "never", "new to", "scared", "is it hard", "what do i need"],
        "regular": ["again", "another session", "pack", "credits", "when next"],
        "solo": ["by myself", "alone", "meet people", "solo players", "no group"],
        "corporate": ["team", "company", "event", "invoice", "how many people"],
        "parent": ["kid", "child", "son", "daughter", "family", "age", "safe"]
    }
    
    message_signals = []
    FOR pattern_type, keywords IN message_patterns:
        IF any(keyword IN user_first_message.lower() FOR keyword IN keywords):
            message_signals.append(pattern_type)
    
    // Confidence scoring
    IF len(message_signals) > 0:
        detected_persona = message_signals[0]
        message_confidence = 0.9
        override = (detected_persona != persona_prior)
    ELSE:
        // No clear pattern, use prior
        detected_persona = persona_prior
        message_confidence = prior_confidence * 0.8  // Slight discount for no signal
        override = false
    
    IF override AND prior_confidence > 0.75:
        // Strong page signal contradicted; ask for clarification
        return {
            persona: detected_persona,
            confidence: message_confidence,
            ask_confirmation: true,
            confirmation_prompt: "Just to confirm — are you looking to {intention}?"
        }
    ELSE:
        return {
            persona: detected_persona,
            confidence: message_confidence,
            ask_confirmation: false,
            override: override
        }

function detect_persona_mid_conversation(conversation_history, latest_message):
    // Trigger: User reveals new intent mid-conversation
    
    original_persona = conversation_history.confirmed_persona
    new_signals = extract_intent_signals(latest_message)
    
    IF new_signals indicate different persona:
        new_persona = new_signals.top_persona
        confidence = new_signals.confidence
        
        // Pivot gracefully
        return {
            persona_changed: true,
            from_persona: original_persona,
            to_persona: new_persona,
            confidence: confidence,
            pivot_message: "Ah I see! Looks like you're looking to {new_intent}. Let me help with that instead..."
        }
    ELSE:
        return {
            persona_changed: false,
            confirmed_persona: original_persona
        }

Friction Detection Algorithm

Pseudo-code for classify_friction(page_events, user_context):


function classify_friction(page_events, user_context):
    // Compile event stream into friction signals
    
    friction_signals = {}
    
    // Signal 1: Offer hesitation
    offer_events = page_events.filter(e => e.type IN ["offer_view", "offer_hesitation"])
    IF len(offer_events) >= 2 AND has_hesitation(offer_events):
        friction_signals["price_hesitation"] = {
            severity: "high",
            confidence: 0.85,
            offer_viewed: offer_events[-1].offer_id,
            hesitation_time: offer_events[-1].dwell_seconds
        }
    
    // Signal 2: Fit ambiguity (repeated FAQ views)
    faq_events = page_events.filter(e => e.type == "faq_view")
    IF len(faq_events) >= 3:
        friction_signals["fit_ambiguity"] = {
            severity: "medium",
            confidence: 0.75,
            topics_viewed: [e.topic FOR e IN faq_events],
            most_common_topic: most_frequent([e.topic FOR e IN faq_events])
        }
    
    // Signal 3: Group coordination friction
    IF "group_organizer" in page_events AND has_form_abandon(page_events, "group_size"):
        friction_signals["group_coordination"] = {
            severity: "high",
            confidence: 0.8,
            abandoned_at_field: "group_size"
        }
    
    // Signal 4: Slot confusion
    schedule_dwell = sum([e.dwell FOR e IN page_events IF e.type == "section_dwell" AND e.section == "schedule"])
    IF schedule_dwell > 15 AND has_form_abandon(page_events):
        friction_signals["slot_confusion"] = {
            severity: "medium",
            confidence: 0.7,
            schedule_dwell_seconds: schedule_dwell
        }
    
    // Signal 5: Safety/trust concerns
    trust_views = page_events.filter(e => e.type IN ["trust_block_view", "trust_badge_view"])
    IF len(trust_views) >= 2 AND has_cta_delay(page_events, delay_threshold=30):
        friction_signals["trust_concern"] = {
            severity: "medium",
            confidence: 0.65,
            trust_checks: len(trust_views)
        }
    
    // Aggregate: return dominant friction type
    IF len(friction_signals) == 0:
        return {
            friction_detected: false,
            confidence: 0.0,
            reason: "No friction signals"
        }
    
    dominant_friction = max(friction_signals.items(), key=lambda x: x[1].severity)
    
    return {
        friction_detected: true,
        primary_friction: dominant_friction[0],
        signals: friction_signals,
        severity: dominant_friction[1].severity,
        confidence: dominant_friction[1].confidence,
        concierge_branch: FRICTION_CONCIERGE_MAP[dominant_friction[0]]
    }

function has_hesitation(offer_events):
    // Check if user viewed offer, then scrolled away without clicking
    FOR event IN offer_events:
        IF event.type == "offer_view" AND event.next_event.type == "scroll_away":
            return true
    return false

function has_form_abandon(events, specific_field=null):
    form_events = events.filter(e => e.type IN ["form_start", "form_abandon"])
    IF len(form_events) == 0: return false
    
    abandon_event = form_events[-1]
    IF abandon_event.type == "form_abandon":
        IF specific_field: return abandon_event.last_field == specific_field
        return true
    return false

function has_cta_delay(events, delay_threshold=30):
    // CTA delay = time between page_load and first CTA click
    load_time = events[0].timestamp  // Assume first event is page_load
    cta_event = events.find(e => e.type == "cta_click")
    
    IF cta_event:
        delay = cta_event.timestamp - load_time
        return delay > delay_threshold
    return true  // No CTA click = delay

10. Nine AI Skill Specifications

Each skill is a micro-service with defined inputs, outputs, and eval targets. All skills feed into the composition engine and persona system.

Skill 1: Venue Profiler

Input: Instagram handle, website URL, Zomato/District link, owner journey answers (5-8 questions)

Process: Scrape IG (photos, captions, followers, engagement), website (menu, pricing, photos, hours), Zomato (sales range, repeat rate, reviews), run Trust × Taste analysis

Output: Venue knowledge graph (70 signals: category, vibe tags, audience hypotheses, visual motifs, pricing structure, social signals, competitive context)

Eval Targets: Template recommendation accuracy (does suggested template match venue reality after 2 weeks?), user approval rate

Skill 2: Campaign Brain

Input: Venue profile, inventory state (which dayparts are weak), benchmarks (peer performance, theme benchmarks), owner preferences

Process: Identify weak inventory slots, recommend audience target (who should we pull this week?), suggest offer elasticity, recommend narrative hook

Output: Weekly campaign brief: {objective, audience_target, offer_recommendation, budget_allocation, narrative_hook_families}

Eval Targets: Walk-in lift vs. control week, offer take rate, audience alignment

Skill 3: Creative OS

Input: Venue assets (photos), campaign brief, primitive type, audience target, creative tier (T1/T2/T3)

Process: Select or generate 5 format variants (static, carousel, reel, story, collection). For T1: select real photos only. For T2: enhance with AI assistance. For T3: generate fully conceptual

Output: 5 ad formats (static, carousel, reel, story, collection) × 3 tiers × audience variations

Eval Targets: CTR per format, completion rate, share rate, audience match accuracy

Skill 4: Offer Engine

Input: Venue type, daypart, audience, weak inventory state, historical offer performance

Process: Analyze price elasticity, recommend offer structure (discount vs. bundle vs. pack vs. trial), estimate take rate

Output: Primary offer + 2-3 alternative offers, with estimated take rates and margin impact

Eval Targets: Actual take rate vs. predicted, conversion lift, margin preservation

Skill 5: WA Concierge (Persona-Aware)

Input: Friction signal, page context, persona confirmation, user message, booking state

Process: Load persona-specific conversation flow, detect objections, adapt offer, guide to booking or escalate

Output: Concierge message, next action (book, offer alternative, transfer to owner)

Eval Targets: Booking rate, conversation turns, user satisfaction, correct persona detection rate

Skill 6: Attribution Joiner

Input: Event stream (ad impression, page visit, WA conversation, booking, show-up)

Process: Join events through user session ID, infer causality (did ad cause walk-in?), handle multi-touch attribution

Output: Attribution chain: {ad_id → page_visit → inquiry → booking → show_up}

Eval Targets: Attribution accuracy (via control test), venue trust in attribution data

Skill 7: Content Gap Requester

Input: Venue profile, Trust × Taste score, current content library, template requirements

Process: Identify missing content (specific photo types, angles, lifestyle shots), recommend shoot list

Output: Actionable shoot list: {shot_type, quantity, priority, reason}

Eval Targets: Owner follow-through rate, quality of content collected

Skill 8: Benchmark Scorer

Input: Venue performance metrics (walk-ins, CTR, conversion, revenue), peer benchmarks, theme benchmarks

Process: Score venue against: last week (trend), peers (percentile), theme (relative performance), time-of-week (day-part intelligence)

Output: Weekly scorecard: {metric, score, percentile, trend, recommendations}

Eval Targets: Actionability (does owner use scorecard recommendations?), accuracy of peer matching

Skill 9: Weekly Owner Summary

Input: Week's performance data (walk-ins, revenue, conversions, top-performing creatives), attribution chains, benchmark scores

Process: Synthesize week into 1-page narrative: what worked, why, what to do next. Highlight top-performing ad and top-converting persona

Output: Beautiful 1-page summary email/in-app card with: top metrics, story of the week, next week's recommendation

Eval Targets: Owner engagement (do they open/read?), do recommendations lead to behavior change?

Consumer-Side Learning

Which page variants convert best? Feed back to creative OS and composition engine:

  • Hero image ranking: Track which hero images produce highest CTR. Rank assets by performance, surface top performers first.
  • Copy testing: Different headlines per variant. Track which headline copy converts best. Update creative brain templates.
  • Offer ranking: Which offers close best? Track by audience persona (beginners prefer trials, regulars prefer packs). Rank offers by take rate.
  • City character: Which city overlays resonate? Delhi vs. Bengaluru show different patterns. Update city overlay personalization.

Owner-Side Learning

Weekly Owner Summary surfaces what worked:

  • Top-performing creative: "Your reel of the open court got 12% CTR — 2x your average"
  • Top-converting persona: "Groups are 3x more likely to book than solos this week"
  • Best daypart: "Thursday 6-8pm is now your strongest slot. Maybe post more Thursday content?"
  • Attribution insight: "Instagram referrals convert at 2x the rate of organic. Should we spend more there?"

Feedback Loop: Learning → Composition


// Pseudo-code: Update composition rules based on learning
function update_composition_rules(week_performance_data):
    // Learn best hero image across all venues in theme
    best_hero_by_theme = {}
    FOR theme IN THEMES:
        variants_in_theme = performance_data.filter(v => v.template == theme)
        best_hero_by_theme[theme] = max(variants_in_theme, key=lambda v: v.ctr).hero_image_asset_id

    // Update composition rule: prefer high-performing hero assets
    COMPOSITION_RULES["hero_selection"].priority_assets = best_hero_by_theme

    // Learn best offers by audience + daypart
    best_offer_by_persona_daypart = {}
    FOR persona, daypart IN cartesian_product(PERSONAS, DAYPARTS):
        matching_conversions = performance_data.filter(
            v => v.persona == persona AND v.daypart == daypart AND v.booked == true
        )
        best_offer = mode(matching_conversions.offer_id)
        best_offer_by_persona_daypart[(persona, daypart)] = best_offer

    // Update composition rule: surface persona-specific offers
    COMPOSITION_RULES["offer_selection"].persona_specific = best_offer_by_persona_daypart

    // Learn city character effectiveness
    best_city_overlay = {}
    FOR city IN CITIES:
        city_variants = performance_data.filter(v => v.city == city)
        best_overlay = max(city_variants, key=lambda v: v.conversion_rate).city_overlay
        best_city_overlay[city] = best_overlay

    COMPOSITION_RULES["city_overlay"].effectiveness = best_city_overlay

    // Push updated rules to composition service
    COMPOSITION_SERVICE.update_rules(COMPOSITION_RULES)
