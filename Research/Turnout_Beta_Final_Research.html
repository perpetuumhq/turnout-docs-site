1. Executive Summary

India's go-out economy is undergoing a fundamental shift: showing up is replacing posting. Real-world presence is becoming a status marker again. This macro signal, validated across 19 research sources (S1-S19), reveals a $2.78 lakh crore media & entertainment sector (2025) with live experiences up 44%, and an $18,864 crore sports economy now legitimized as a commercial category.

The Opportunity: Turnout enters an India market where consumers increasingly value experience over inventory (61% of diners), social wellness (Gen Z treats wellness as identity), and structured group activities (pickleball, trainer-led classes, interactive dining). These categories combine:

  • Repeatable, monetizable behavior (slots, reservations, tickets, packs)
  • Strong social sharability and proof signals
  • Clear WhatsApp coordination touchpoints (scheduling, group planning, payment splits)
  • Trust-rich narratives (trainer credentials, venue reputation, attendee photos)

Turnout's Beta Thesis: Build a mobile-first platform that converts discovery into footfall by making three decisions easy: where (curated venue pages), when (availability + social scheduling), and with whom (group coordination + WhatsApp handoff). Do not build a horizontal listing layer. Build category-specific page templates and WA journeys around commercial primitives (slot, reservation, deposit, pack, trial, membership).

Wave 1 (0-60 days): Delhi NCR, Bangalore, Mumbai. Focus on three categories: sports-social, brunch/cafe dwell, wellness/self-care. These balance cultural heat, repeatability, and clear closure primitives.

2. India Go-Out Economy: Macro Signals

The Macro Shift: Presence > Posting

[S1] Fortune India's coverage of Zomato's "District by Zomato" 2026 report frames real-world presence as a reset: presence is the new status marker. This is not nostalgia. It reflects algorithmic fatigue and a deliberate move toward intentional offline behavior. For Turnout, this means the value proposition is not "discover a new restaurant"; it is "move from uncertainty to commitment."

Experience Is Winning Over Plain Inventory

[S2] ET HospitalityWorld reported that 61% of Indian diners value interactive elements—open kitchens, chef interactions—more than transactional food purchase. This signifies that the experience layer is now a core product attribute, not decoration. Consumers are rewarding restaurants that prioritize participation, narrative, and social proof. Turnout's page design must emphasize what makes the room feel different, not just what's on the menu.

Wellness Has Gone Social

[S3] The Economic Times noted that Gen Z treats wellness—pickleball, dance workouts, low-alcohol formats—as social identity, not solitary optimization. This makes wellness shareable and group-compatible, multiplying both traffic and word-of-mouth. Community fitness (trainers, group classes, workshops) thus becomes commercially stronger than it appears in traditional wellness categories.

Sports-Social is Now a Real Business

[S4] IBEF highlighted pickleball's commercial appeal: low setup cost, fast break-even in metro cities, expansion into housing, malls, and corporates. [S6] India's sports economy crossed ₹18,864 crore in 2025, signaling mature investor appetite.

For Turnout: sports-social (pickleball, padel, running clubs, cycling) combines three killer attributes:

  • Repeat behavior (not one-off) — users come back weekly or bi-weekly
  • Visible proof (photos, match scores, leaderboards) — naturally shareable
  • Group coordination (teams, splits, scheduling) — native WhatsApp handoff moment

Live Experiences Regained Economic Momentum

[S5] FICCI-EY reported India's media and entertainment sector growing to ₹2.78 lakh crore in 2025, with live events up 44%. This is not a trend; it is institutional validation that experience spending is material and recurring, not anecdotal one-offs.

City-Specific Signals Drive Localization

[S7-S19] A rich mosaic of city-level signals from Restaurant India, ET Hospitality World, TOI bureaus, and culture reporters confirms that cafe culture, sports behavior, dining formats, and wellness preferences vary dramatically by city. Delhi NCR rewards abundance and utility. Mumbai rewards lifestyle identity. Bengaluru rewards community loops. Each city's opening thesis must differ. (See Section 4: City × Theme Heatmap.)

  • Delhi NCR: Lead with abundance-breaking (sharp curation). Sports, fitness, brunch dominate. Users want to know "what's best today," not "what's new."

  • Bengaluru: Lead with community proof. Creator attribution, local partnerships, group invites. Multiple overlapping communities; each needs tailored proof.

6. PLG Pattern Analysis: Five Viral Mechanics

The 39 benchmarks reveal five repeating patterns for product-led growth in India. Turnout should adopt all five:

Pattern 1: Output is the Ad (Canva, Loom)

Mechanism: User creates artifact (design, video) → artifact is beautiful/shareable → user shares result → new user wants to create own artifact → signs up to Canva/Loom.

Turnout Application: Every booking completion should generate a shareable artifact:

  • Sports-social: Match scorecard, leaderboard placement screenshot, team roster card
  • Brunch: "Group plan card" with attendees, time, restaurant, estimated cost
  • Fitness: Class streak badge, trainer progress card, session photo with group
  • Wellness: Therapist session summary, wellness ritual card (date, service, therapist)
  • Creator Events: Attendance certificate, creator co-host card, event recap photo

These cards should be: frame-worthy, branded (include Turnout logo), instant (generated on booking completion), and shareable (WhatsApp, Instagram, group chat).

Pattern 2: Instant-Output Virality (Loom, NotebookLM)

Mechanism: Action completes → instant beautiful output → sharing is effortless → viral loop triggers.

Turnout Application: On booking confirmation, show:

  • Instant group plan visualization (who's coming, when, cost-per-person)
  • One-tap WhatsApp share: "Join me for [activity] at [place] [time] — [link]"
  • Leaderboard position update (if sports/fitness): "You're #3 this week!"
  • Trainer introduction card (if fitness/wellness): "Your trainer is [name], certified [cert], 500+ 5-star reviews"

Pattern 3: Free-Hook-to-Paid Upsell (Discotech, ClassPass)

Mechanism: Free tier entry (RSVP, trial, free booking) → user sees premium option (VIP table, advanced class, full package) → low-friction upgrade → $X conversion.

Turnout Application: Design offer ladders for each category:

  • Sports: Free group formation + RSVP → paid court booking → pack credits (10% discount)
  • Fitness: Free class discovery → trial pass ($1-2) → 5-class pack (10% discount) → monthly membership (15% discount)
  • Wellness: Free therapist browsing → booking → 5-service pack (12% discount) → subscription (20% discount)
  • Dining: Free reservation → optional deposit (confirms intent) → group meal bundle (15% discount)

Upsell should NOT feel salesy. Lead with value: "Save ₹300 with a 5-pack" or "Get ₹150 back with a quarterly membership."

Pattern 4: Embedded Virality (Calendly, Discord, Stripe)

Mechanism: User shares booking/group link with friend → friend clicks → friend joins Turnout → new user re-shares to others → network effects.

Turnout Application: Make sharing the path to conversion:

  • Group booking: User creates group (2+ people) → generates group booking link → "Share with friends" is top CTA → friends click → friends join Turnout → group cohesion increases
  • Team recurring bookings: "Weekly badminton crew" repeats every Wednesday → user invites 2 more → recurring group drives frequency
  • Referral virality: User refers friend to Turnout → friend books → referrer gets ₹100 Turnout credit → compounding referral loops

Pattern 5: Workflow Lock-in (Zapier, Notion, Figma)

Mechanism: User embeds Turnout into their routine (auto-reminders, group split-pay, leaderboard tracking) → workflow becomes dependent → switching costs rise → lifetime value compounds.

Turnout Application: Automate the routine:

  • Weekly reminders: "Your regular badminton matches start next Wednesday at 6 PM"
  • Auto-payment splits: "Your group owes ₹1,500 for court rental — splitting equally 3 ways" → auto-debit option
  • Leaderboard tracking: Every match auto-updates ranking; weekly digest: "You jumped to #12 this week"
  • Trainer continuity: "Book your next session with Priya" → one-tap reschedule
  • Group history: "You've attended 47 sessions with this group" → social proof of commitment

7. GTM Filter: 6 Evaluation Criteria

Every design decision, category launch, and feature prioritization should pass through this 6-point filter. It is the founder's north star for Wave 1 coherence.

Criterion 1: Consumer Heat (Evidence Threshold)

Definition: Is this format visibly gaining consumer participation and mention NOW, not theoretically? Test: Can you find evidence in Q1 2026 coverage (S1-S19) showing current consumer adoption?

Turnout Application:

  • Pass ✓: Sports-social (pickleball boom S4, S6), interactive dining (61% of diners S2), community fitness (Gen Z movement identity S3), creator events (regional culture shift S16, S19)
  • Fail ✗: NFT-gated events (zero consumer heat in Q1 2026), metaverse fitness (zero adoption signals), blockchain ticketing (absent from sources)

Criterion 2: Monetization Clarity (Commercial Primitive Test)

Definition: Does this category have a clear, repeatable commercial primitive? Can you name the unit of sale without ambiguity?

Turnout Application:

  • Pass ✓: Sports (slot booking = ₹500-1000 per slot), Brunch (reservation + deposit = ₹1000-2000), Fitness (class = ₹300-500, pack = ₹1200-1800)
  • Fail ✗: "Vibe discovery" (what's the unit?), "Aesthetic wellness" (what's the charge?), "Adventure networking" (too vague, no clear transaction)

Criterion 3: WhatsApp Fit (Coordination Test)

Definition: After a user books, do they naturally hand off to WhatsApp for group coordination, payment splits, logistics? Or does the page answer everything and the user never re-engages?

Turnout Application:

  • Pass ✓: Sports (organize teams, confirm attendees), Brunch (who's driving, table preference, dietary notes), Fitness (buddy accountability, trainer messages)
  • Fail ✗: Solo spa bookings (user goes alone; no group coordination), movie tickets (logistics handled by cinema, no WA handoff), quick grab-and-go cafes (no coordination needed)

Criterion 4: Repeatability (Frequency Test)

Definition: Will a user come back to this category within 7-30 days? Or is this a one-off, holiday-only, or annual event?

Turnout Application:

  • Pass ✓: Weekly sports bookings, bi-weekly fitness, fortnightly brunch, monthly wellness package
  • Fail ✗: Annual music festivals, once-a-year wellness retreats, quarterly team-building events (too sparse; not repeatable habit)

Criterion 5: Shareability (Proof Artifact Test)

Definition: Does the experience generate a natural artifact the user wants to share? Photos, leaderboards, testimonials, certificates, group compositions?

Turnout Application:

  • Pass ✓: Sports matches (player photos, scorecards, leaderboards), Brunch (group photo, restaurant ambiance), Creator events (attendee proof, creator co-host card), Fitness (before-after, badge, group class photo)
  • Fail ✗: Solo therapist appointments (private; no share impulse), document scanning services, tax consultations (no visual proof story)

Criterion 6: Proof Richness (Data Compounding Test)

Definition: Will the product learn from each transaction in a way that compounds trust for the next user? Can you build a confidence score (trainer ratings, venue reliability, group attendance) that gets stronger over time?

Turnout Application:

  • Pass ✓: Trainer certifications + student reviews, Venue reliability (on-time starts, clean courts), Creator reputation (attendee satisfaction, group loyalty), Sports player profile (ranking, win-loss, teammate loyalty)
  • Fail ✗: One-off festival bookings (no repeating entities to rate), random experience discovery (no recurring supply-side profile to build), anonymous events (no reputation accumulation)

9. Commercial Primitive Stack: 7 Layers × 40+ Primitives

These are the monetization and coordination units the product MUST support. Do not launch without full coverage.

Layer 1: Discovery & Access (5 primitives)

  • Deep link / QR arrival: User lands from ad, creator story, venue bio with context already known.
  • Area page: Neighborhood or theme clusters (e.g., "Pickleball courts in Indiranagar") without generic listing.
  • Creator / referrer context: "Priya referred you" → preserves trust path.
  • Waitlist: For invite-led or limited-inventory experiences.
  • RSVP / expression of interest: Softer than booking; useful for soft-launch experiences.

Layer 2: Decision & Capacity (5 primitives)

  • Reservation: Fixed date/time/party size; requires confirmation.
  • Slot / session: Class time, court timeslot, trainer availability.
  • Ticket / guestlist entry: Event name, entry slot, capacity cutoff.
  • Deposit / token hold: Signals seriousness without full payment.
  • Table upgrade / experience layer: Second-step choice after base booking (e.g., window seat, premium room).

Layer 3: Packaging & Pricing (7 primitives)

  • Trial: Low-risk first purchase (₹1-2 fitness class, ₹0 RSVP with optional upgrade).
  • Drop-in: Single-visit, no commitment, minimal friction.
  • Pack / bundle: 3, 5, 10-unit bundle at 10-15% discount. Increases AOV and repeat.
  • Membership / credits: Recurring monthly charge for unlimited (fitness) or monthly credit allocation (sports).
  • Off-peak offer: Discounts for low-demand times (Tuesday morning yoga, Wednesday court slots).
  • Corporate / group package: Team bookings, celebrations, corporate outings.
  • Gift / voucher: Physical or digital gift card. Powers referral + occasion moments.

Layer 4: Trust & Reassurance (7 primitives)

  • Verified media & provenance: Show what's real (trainer cert, venue license), what's AI-enhanced, what's owner-confirmed.
  • Safety cues: Women-safe, beginner-safe, privacy-safe, family-safe markers.
  • What to expect: Dress code, session intensity, parking, kids policy, accessibility, pet policy.
  • Refund / cancellation / no-show: Human-readable policy. "Free cancellation up to 6 hours" not legalese.
  • Social proof: Testimonials, attendance norms, trainer/creator reputation, group loyalty metrics.
  • Beginner-friendly: "Great for first-timers," "No equipment needed," "Community welcomes solo joiners."
  • Proof richness: Session photos, trainer credentials, group composites, leaderboard standing.

Layer 5: Social & Coordination (5 primitives)

  • Invite group: "Add your crew." User can input names, get WhatsApp links auto-generated.
  • Split pay: "Cost is ₹500 total, ₹250 per person." Auto-splits, facilitates group Venmo-like behavior.
  • Share plan card: One-tap artifact: attendees, time, location, cost breakdown, trainer intro.
  • Creator attribution: "Thanks [Creator Name] for bringing you in." Preserves narrative context and referrer memory.
  • Buddy / bring-a-friend: Commercial primitive masquerading as social: "Bring a friend free" = acquisition driver.

Layer 6: Closure & Retention (5 primitives)

  • Booking confirmation: Screenshot-friendly (WhatsApp-ready) with all details and countdown.
  • Reminder: 24h and 2h before. Time, location, what to bring, re-confirmation option.
  • Check-in: Moment of truth: "You've arrived." Photo moment, attendance proof, community signal.
  • Post-visit rebook: "Book your next session" → one-tap for recurring categories. Friction-free repeat.
  • Opt-in subscription / loyalty: Only ask AFTER first successful session, not upfront. "Love this trainer? Subscribe for ₹1500/month" feels earned, not pushy.

Layer 7: Merchant Control & Explainability (6 primitives)

  • Approval / reject: Venue owner approves page, offers, creative packets before going live.
  • Content gap request: System tells owner: "You're missing photos of court 2. Add 3 photos to boost conversion by 15%."
  • Offer ladder: Owner-approved pricing tiers. No opaque algorithmic pricing. "Trial ₹1, Pack-5 ₹1200, Monthly ₹1800."
  • Capacity override: Venue can block dates, slots, or inventory windows if temporarily unavailable.
  • Proof of outcome: System attribution: "This page, this message path, or this referrer caused this booking." Owner learns why it worked.
  • Media refresh request: "Your photos are 8 weeks old. Refresh to stay competitive. Updated pages see 20% higher conversion."

10. Key Strategic Insights for Beta

Founder Guardrails: Where to Start

  • Start with 1-2 categories in Wave 1, not 5. Depth > breadth. Get sports-social or brunch to "beautiful" first, then scale supply. Trying to do everything results in nothing working.
  • Start with categories that have natural WhatsApp handoff. If the page answers everything and the user never opens WhatsApp again, you've built Resy clone, not a coordination platform.
  • Start by recruiting supply, not demand. Get 5-10 venues or trainers to 100+ monthly bookings (beautiful data) before running paid acquisition. Proof compounds faster if supply is obsessed with Turnout.
  • Start with a single city per vertical. Don't launch sports in Delhi, Pune, and Bangalore simultaneously. Go deep in Delhi, prove the moat, then template for Bangalore.

What to Avoid in Wave 1

  • Do not build a horizontal listing layer. "Discover any activity" is Insider. You are not Insider. You are category-specific + proof-rich + coordination-native.
  • Do not over-index on nightlife complexity first. Table minimums, promoter logic, event irregularity will swallow product focus. Save for Wave 2.
  • Do not begin with opaque dynamic pricing. Lead with merchant-approved offer ladders (trial, pack, subscription). Opacity is trust risk; India's consumers are price-sensitive.
  • Do not treat WhatsApp as a broadcast CRM. Avoid sending ₹10-off discount blasts to 100K users weekly. WhatsApp is for coordination (group invites, payment splits, reminders). Use it statefully, not spray-and-pray.
  • Do not fake venue visuals or promise false outcomes. Truthful creative (not AI-enhanced) is a core principle. If a court is not photogenic, own that and emphasize the community/experience instead. Trust compounds; deception decays.

What WhatsApp Is Good At

  • Group formation: "Create a recurring Tuesday badminton crew" → invite links → auto-group formation → split pay calculated → weekly reminder sent.
  • Coordination after booking: "Your group needs to confirm dietary preferences for Sunday brunch" → one-tap form link → group response consolidation.
  • Proof sharing: "Your leaderboard placement" → share to group chat → friends see you ranked #5 → virality loop activates.
  • Trusted reminder: Not a sales channel; a logistical channel. "Your trainer Priya's class starts in 2 hours, Sector 14, Fort Kochi. See you?"
  • Payment coordination: "You owe ₹450 for court rental; split 3 ways = ₹150 each" → auto-Venmo-like settlement.

What WhatsApp is NOT Good At

  • Broadcast marketing. Sending unsolicited discount blasts to 100K users. This will tank retention and invite regulatory scrutiny.
  • Cold acquisition. Don't buy phone lists and spam WhatsApp new users. Organic (referral, creator-led) is Turnout's native motion.
  • Transaction completion. WhatsApp payment link feels slimy for repeat use. Web + WhatsApp handoff is better than WA-native all-the-way.

Trust Risks to Mitigate Early

  • Venue reputation fraud: Fake 5-star reviews, bot check-ins, AI-generated visuals. Audit photos and reviews rigorously in Wave 1. A single deceptive venue destroys category trust.
  • Trainer credential gaps: Listing trainers without verifying certifications. This is India's highest-trust signal. Verify certs before listing; update annually.
  • No-show cascades: If users RSVP and don't show, supply gets demotivated (empty courts, cancelled classes). Charge no-show fees early. Skin in the game matters.
  • Opaque pricing: Hidden service fees, surprise charges, algorithmic variable pricing. Lead with transparency. "Fee: ₹0. Turnout takes 12% commission." Explain clearly.
  • Privacy breaches: Listing user data (attendance, trainer preferences, payment info) without consent. GDPR-like stance in India will differentiate Turnout; encode privacy early.

Design Rules for Beta

  • Lead with proof, not aesthetics. A grainy photo of real attendees > a pristine AI render. Users trust reality; they distrust polish without substance.
  • Page should answer four questions quickly: "Why this place?" (narrative/experience), "Why now?" (timing/vibe), "Why for me?" (tailoring to user), "What exactly happens next?" (clear next step).
  • Every page should generate a shareable artifact. Booking confirmation → beautiful card (attendees, time, cost, trainer intro) → share to WhatsApp. This is your viral loop.
  • Onboarding should take <2 minutes per category. User sees category → picks theme (sports, fitness, brunch, etc.) → sees top-3 venues → taps one → sees slots → books. Friction = churn.
  • No infinite scroll. Show top-5 venues (curated by GTM filter), not 50. More choice = higher friction. Let supply compete to be top-5, not top-50.

Synthesis: The Beta North Star

Turnout is not a discovery app. Turnout is not a reservation widget. Turnout is a coordination + proof platform for group experiences. Users come for curation (where), stay for community (who), and return for habit (when). Design every feature through this lens:

  • Curation: Top-3-5 venues per category per city, not 50. Merchant-approved pages with proof artifacts (photos, trainers, testimonials).
  • Coordination: Group formation, split pay, schedule sync, attendee confirmation. Native WhatsApp handoff for logistics.
  • Proof: Leaderboards, badges, trainer credentials, testimonials, attendance norms, group history. Trust through compounding visibility.
  • Habit: Reminders, recurrence, streaks, subscriptions. Friction-free repeat bookings.

If a feature does not serve one of these four pillars, defer it. Beta is the time to be ruthless.
